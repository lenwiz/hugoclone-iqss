A Love of Learning and Helping Others Succeed: Wendy Guan Retires

February 27, 2025

Home › Research Resources › A Love of Learning and Helping Others Succeed: Wendy Guan Retires

By Colleen Walsh

A map covered in red dots hangs on Wendy Guan’s office wall. The dense rosy points obscure what lies beneath until a closer look reveals Italy, Sicily, and the tip of Tunisia.

Those vibrant points enabled Harvard’s Francis Goelet Professor of Medieval History Michael McCormick to connect the dots. He had turned to Guan and her team at Harvard’s IQSS Center for Geographic Analysis (CGA) to help track the path of the Plague of Justinian (541–ca. 750 CE) for his medieval history class.

“I was overjoyed when hearing him presenting his findings with our mapping tools,” Guan said, describing how plague occurrences appeared to coincide with popular trade routes, suggesting merchants carried the disease along ancient Roman roads.

The map represents just one example of Guan’s nearly 20 years of work as CGA executive director. She helped scholars, students, and experts across Harvard and beyond use geospatial analysis software and datasets to understand the world and address pressing problems. Guan retired at the end of February 2025.

“To see something come out of our work was always so rewarding,” Guan said, explaining the joy of watching others take CGA data and produce amazing discoveries. She described CGA’s strength as building databases and coordinating data applications to help others research more effectively, while facilitating critical connections within Harvard and with outside institutions.

Some connections helped save lives. By harvesting data in collaboration with Boston University, Guan’s team provided structural maps aiding first responders during a 7.3 earthquake in Haiti in 2010, and offered information following the 2011 earthquake and tsunami in Fukushima, Japan. They tracked COVID’s spread, assisted students harvesting social media tweets tied to the Arab Spring, and aided scholars exploring climate change-driven migration from Africa to Europe using cell phone data.

Other CGA-supported work illuminated history and humanities. One project Guan recalls fondly involved tracing fluctuations in religious practices in a South American country by charting spikes in rentals of religious-themed outfits for Catholic and Protestant holidays. Harvard researcher Rachel McCleary had thousands of records from costume rental stores. Using CGA’s tools, she could see shifting popularity over a 30-year period on a digital screen.

“The map was flashing, dancing back and forth,” Guan remembered with a smile. “Studying religion. I would never have dreamed of doing that before I came here to Harvard.”

Breaking New Ground

The CGA was created in 2006 to support research and teaching across all University disciplines related to geospatial technology and methods. The center combines consultation services, technical training, platform development, and sponsored research.

IQSS director and Albert J. Weatherhead III University Professor Gary King said Guan “helped build our Center for Geographic Analysis” and “has inspired other universities to build similar GIS groups.” He noted she “built CGA into a powerhouse and made a big difference here, as well as nationally and even internationally.”

King called Guan “utterly reliable, completely unflappable, impossible to provoke,” and “exactly the kind of person you would want to lead a major institution.” He said her contribution to the institution was extraordinary, often made behind the scenes. Guan preferred keeping a low profile, giving visibility to others and empowering them. King said he sometimes learned about her projects through the news media.

“She made it possible for everybody to discover, preserve and distribute knowledge in better ways than they could otherwise,” King said, adding that when students came with related questions, he’d simply say to find Wendy at CGA.

A Life on the Move

Guan’s gravitation toward geography seems fitting given her life defined by significant moves — some planned, others beyond her control.

Born in China in 1961 to academics who taught literature at Beijing University, Guan’s family was soon upended by China’s Cultural Revolution. As academics, her parents were considered threats to the proletariat under a 1966 government decree. They were fired and exiled to the countryside for “reeducation” through hard labor.

One memory stands out from those early days: watching Red Guards raid her childhood home and burn her family’s books in the courtyard. Allowed access to only one room for personal items, young Guan protested, wanting to retrieve a favorite stuffed toy. Her parents warned her “not to even think about trying to open that door.”

After a 1,200-mile journey from Beijing by boat, rail, bus, and donkey to her parents’ ancestral home near Hong Kong, the family became rice farmers, plowing fields with oxen. A brief teenage visit to Beijing, funded by her mother’s recovered savings, left its mark.

“I could see the transition, the difference in the geography and the way people lived,” Guan said, “and I was so intrigued by that.”

As the child of professors, Guan learned that rigorous study was prized. She became an avid reader and learned English through Voice of America broadcasts on a radio her parents purchased with all their savings. Listening to such an “enemy’s radio station” risked prison or worse; Guan witnessed nearby teachers sentenced to decades behind bars for doing so.

“Every year people were pulled out of their homes, rounded up and sent to prison, or shot.”

Guan found light in learning. When Mao died in 1977 and China gradually opened, she took college entrance exams and became one of only 50 students from tens of thousands in her county to attend university. The country’s secrecy made studying maps, geographic imagery, and aerial photos impossible for her. She majored in biology at South China Normal University in Guangzhou and pursued ecology in Peking University’s geography department for graduate studies, where she “sat in on a lot of geography classes.”

After receiving her master’s degree, Guan joined Tsinghua University’s new urban planning institute, teaching urban ecology and doing research. But she longed to travel beyond China’s borders. In 1984, when travel restrictions eased, she acted.

“I didn’t care where I went or what I studied. I was going to squeeze through the crack in the door for fear that it would shut again.”

A New Life in a New Country from Coast to Coast

Guan landed in Ontario, Canada for a second master’s degree in Canadian heritage and development studies at Trent University, then moved to Georgia for a PhD in ecology at the University of Georgia’s Institute of Ecology led by Eugene Odum, widely known as “the Father of Modern Ecology.” She planned to finish and return to China. Then came news of Tiananmen Square.

“The Tiananmen massacre changed everything,” Guan recalled, her voice breaking as she remembered lost friends and dashed hopes for her country.

Guan pivoted from environmental modeling to geographic information systems, recognizing she “needed to get a job in the US because I wasn’t going back, and GIS was a booming field.” After postdoctoral work at SUNY Buffalo, she moved to Florida’s South Florida Water Management District as a geographer leading the GIS team. She married, had two children, and acquired database management, system design, and programming skills.

Her next role took her to Seattle with a multinational corporation in forestry and conservation. Though the position fit well, Guan struggled as often the only female, nonwhite person under 50 in the room. When the company reorganized, she was pushed out.

While working for a GIS consulting firm and planning to return to Florida, a job listing caught her eye. “It was a listing for a job in Cambridge, miles away from Florida,” she said. “But I thought it’s Harvard, I have to take a look.” The description matched her “dream job.”

For CGA founding director Peter Bol, Guan was a dream candidate. He noted she had good GIS experience, industry background, and a PhD in the field, calling her “very, very thorough, very hard-working, and very fair.”

Guan immediately tackled Bol’s three priorities: supporting research using spatial analysis, setting up training programs, and connecting with faculty across the university. “Wendy saw what we needed to do,” Bol said, “and started to figure out ways to do it.”

Sumeeta Srinivasan, who interviewed for a CGA preceptor position in 2006, most remembers Guan’s kindness. Juggling work and childcare of two young children, Srinivasan got the interview time wrong. “Wendy was very kind and rescheduled and I just remember that kindness,” said Srinivasan, now a Tufts professor. Guan also supported her through early-stage breast cancer during her tenure. “Wendy held my hand through it because I think she was the only one I told.”

In April 2020, when students volunteered to do geographic analysis for COVID-19 response, Guan set them up with software and tutored them in methodology — all on her own time, said CGA GIS services manager Jeff Blossom. By March 2021, the Coronavirus Visualization Team had more than 600 members working on dozens of projects.

Blossom, who worked under Guan since 2007, called her a “dream” manager. “No problem was too big for her, and she really had the CGA at her heart,” he said, noting her dedication even in final months to ensure a smooth transition.

Learning, Looking Back and Next Steps

Guan laughs recalling her technology evolution — from Tsinghua University’s cumbersome machine with a mechanical arm manually placing map points to today’s sleek laptop running complex mapping software. Colleagues routinely cite her ongoing desire to stay current with geospatial innovations.

“She was constantly learning,” said Bol, recalling finding her repeatedly on webinars to keep abreast of new developments.

“Wendy knows so much about technology in general,” said Blossom, “and keeps on top of everything, geospatial or otherwise.”

Looking back, Guan said she “went from biology to ecology to geography, to environmental modeling, and from being a power user of the technology and a practitioner, to an enabler.” She calls the Harvard professors “enablers” too, because they not only use technology but enhance the tools.

She considers colleagues, professors, scholars, and students deep sources of inspiration. Their passion was infectious — every morning she looked forward to work. Before Harvard, she had no concept of using GIS to study public health, humanities, history, social justice, or business.

“I’ve learned so much from my colleagues and I’ve loved it.”

Next, the mother of two will relocate to the West Coast’s Bay Area to help care for her new grandson near her daughter.

“That’s my next dream job,” said Guan.
