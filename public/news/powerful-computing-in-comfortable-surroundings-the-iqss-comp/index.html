Powerful computing in comfortable surroundings: the IQSS Computer Labs

May 24, 2023

Home › Research Resources › Powerful computing in comfortable surroundings: the IQSS Computer Labs

by Danielle Benaroche Gottesman

Nestled within Harvard’s Knafel Building—known primarily for government and international studies—is a high-end computing refuge. It’s always open and filled with cutting-edge technology and software.

“Most computer labs have gray walls and feel very plain,” says IQSS Computer Lab Manager Randy Kiessig. “We have a colorful atmosphere, beautiful wood desks, and the winter garden—which feels a lot like a coffee shop.”

Students have long benefited from on-campus computer labs, but pandemic-era lifestyle changes shifted user habits toward personal computer use in isolated spaces. The labs aim to meet the full spectrum of needs for Harvard students, staff, and faculty, offering both pleasing and practical environments for focused work.

Open to anyone with a Harvard ID card, as well as MIT-affiliated patrons, the Lab features the most powerful computers on campus. Kiessig describes it as “the only [computer] Lab available to everyone at Harvard.” The labs see graduate students, medical school students, GSD students, and government students at all hours.

“The top two ways people hear about us are from walking in,” Kiessig says, “or they’ll hear from friends about how good a lab it is.” The labs are open 24 hours a day, staffed in person and online.

The Four Labs

The facility includes four fully functioning labs:

  • Concourse Lab
  • K301
  • Computer Training Lab
  • Government Computer Lab (for government graduate students)

Each offers varying capabilities with access to specialized equipment exceeding typical personal computers. The labs house powerful workstations with a minimum of 32GB of memory, two TB SSD hard drives, specialized software, and large monitors—all maintained by trained Harvard IT professionals. On-site technical support staff helps users troubleshoot issues and answer questions.

Collaborative and Quiet Spaces

The labs offer quiet areas (such as the Fung Library) for solo projects as well as collaborative spaces. From the winter meditation garden in the atrium to the K301 Lab, users can convene in various areas for group projects or research brainstorming.

Security and Connectivity

The labs provide fast, reliable internet essential for processing large files or significant data. Security is taken seriously—lab computers are protected by antivirus software, and Harvard’s network provides additional security measures. Photography, video, and audio recording devices are not permitted, ensuring users’ content is safeguarded.

Specialized Equipment

The labs feature a high-velocity scanner that can process thousands of pages in minutes with OCR capability scanning into 15 languages to create searchable PDFs.

Notable machines include:

  • Mac Pro: 28-core Intel machine with 192 GB of RAM and a 32" Apple Pro Display XDR
  • AMD Ryzen Threadripper PRO: 24 cores and 128 GB of memory
  • Dell workstation: specialized video card for statistical work with a 45" display

Printing is available in each lab for anyone with a University Crimson Print account, in various size and color formats.

User-Friendly Features

“We have standing desks, and a docking station just for iPads,” says Kiessig. Users can also connect their own PCs to larger monitors with external keyboards and ethernet access.

Kiessig notes that “The STATA package (statistical analysis software) that would take two days to run on a laptop will run in 30 minutes on our Mac Pro,” which he describes as the only computer of its kind accessible to everyone on campus.

Additional Resources

The labs offer items for checkout including overhead projectors, a Yeti USB microphone for recording podcasts and interviews, and as Kiessig puts it, “pretty much every kind of cable and converter you would ever need.”

Intangible benefits include increased productivity, comfort, convenience, and consistent structure for organized workflow.

“We try to be open to what people need and are always implementing feedback. If we can, we do,” Kiessig added. “Like all of Harvard, we want it to feel like a safe space to work and study in, and everyone should feel comfortable here.”
