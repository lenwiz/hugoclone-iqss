<div class="hwp-text-block field field--name-field-hwp-body field--type-text-long field--label-hidden"><p>Richard S. Grossman is Andrews Professor of Economics at Wesleyan University in Middletown, CT and a Visiting Scholar at the Institute for Quantitative Social Science at Harvard University. He is the author of <em>Unsettled Account: The Evolution of Banking in the Industrialized World Since 1800</em> (Princeton University Press, 2010) and <em>WRONG: Nine Economic Policy Disasters and What We Can Learn from Them</em> (Oxford University Press, 2013). He teaches classes in American and European Economic History, Macroeconomics, and Money and Banking. He graduated <em>magna cum laude</em> from Harvard College, holds an MScEcon degree in Economic History from the London School of Economics and Political Science of the University of London, and A.M. and PhD degrees in Economics from Harvard University. Prior to joining the faculty at Wesleyan, he worked as an international economist at the United States Department of State.  He has held visiting faculty appointments at Harvard, Yale, and the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, and has received research support from the National Science Foundation and the German Marshall Fund of the United States, and was a 2013 Fellow of the John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation. He has worked and consulted on Wall Street and has testified as an expert witness in federal court. He has been published in the American Economic Review, Journal of Economic History, Journal of Money, Credit, and Banking, and Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis, and other scholarly journals. His op-eds have appeared in the Wall Street Journal, Boston Globe, USA Today, and other newspapers and he has appeared on local, national, and foreign radio and TV. He is a Research Fellow at the<a href="http://cepr.org/"> Centre for Economic Policy Research</a> in London and a Research Network Fellow of <a href="http://www.cesifo-group.de/ifoHome.html">CESifo</a> in Munich. He is a member of the International Advisory Board of the <a href="https://www.quceh.org.uk/">Queen’s University Centre for Economic History, Belfast</a>, and an Advisor and Research Steering Group member at the Irish <a href="https://www.quceh.org.uk/">Centre for Economics, Policy, and History</a>.</p></div>
