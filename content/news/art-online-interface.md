*By Colleen Walsh*

**New desktop application streamlines connection between IQSS's Dataverse and Sid**

A new application called LOOP, developed at Harvard's Institute for Quantitative Social Science (IQSS), aims to simplify the workflow for researchers who want to analyze data from the Harvard Dataverse using Sid—Harvard's version of the open-source Open OnDemand dashboard that connects scholars with supercomputers.

Len Wisniewski, senior director of research computing at IQSS, leads the Sid team that co-designed the LOOP application with the Dataverse team. The software targets researchers across disciplines—from political science to psychology to the physical sciences.

"By simplifying the process of sharing files between Dataverse and research computing systems, researchers can then spend more time focusing on their research," Wisniewski explained. He noted that requiring extra steps to access data creates friction that discourages researchers from leveraging data sharing on Dataverse.

Wisniewski described how researchers often say they are doing one task and want to move to another, but face the burden of learning an entirely separate process to access data. The goal in developing LOOP is to meet basic user requirements while avoiding unnecessary technical tasks.

## The Problem LOOP Solves

Previously, researchers wanting to study trends or evaluate data hosted on the Harvard Dataverse repository needed to:

1. Download data onto their computers
2. Upload it to the FASRC clusters to run analysis
3. Download results from Sid back to their computers
4. Upload findings back into Dataverse

This four-step process required time, patience, and familiarity with both systems.

## The New Workflow

With LOOP, accessed via a drop-down menu from Sid, scholars can seamlessly move files between Dataverse and Sid directly from laptops, phones, desktops, tablets, or potentially even their Teslas.

"That's the idea, ubiquitous computing," Wisniewski said. "You could do all your research work anytime, anywhere, even from your car while driving across the desert, as long as you have an internet connection."

Wisniewski calls the single dashboard of Open OnDemand (Sid) "the holy grail of high-performance computing." High-performance computing has traditionally required users to deeply understand the complex underlying subsystems of supercomputers.

"Researchers will now have this single place where they do all their research, and when they're done they can just go home," he said, rather than choreographing applications and data among multiple environments.

## Beta Testing

Currently in its beta phase, Wisniewski and his team are seeking volunteers to test LOOP. "We'll immediately benefit from more user experience research," he said, hoping to assess the application's usefulness and ease of use with early adopters and identify additional features users might want.

---

*Sid is named in memory of Sidney Verba, a political science professor known for research on democratic participation and political inequality, and for his efforts unifying Harvard University Libraries and digitizing parts of their online catalogue for open access.*

*The Dataverse Project was founded by IQSS Director Gary King in 2006. Its software platform provides preservation and archival infrastructure, allowing researchers to share, control, and receive recognition for their data through a web browser interface.*
