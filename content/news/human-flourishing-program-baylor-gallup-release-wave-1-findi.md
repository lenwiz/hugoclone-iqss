The Global Flourishing Study (GFS), a multi-year collaborative research effort conducted by the Human Flourishing Program at Harvard University, Baylor's Institute for Studies of Religion, Gallup, and the Center for Open Science, has released its First Wave findings on human flourishing. This represents one of the most comprehensive studies of human well-being ever undertaken, defining flourishing as "living in a state in which all aspects of a person's life are good."

The study is led by principal investigators Tyler J. VanderWeele, Director of the Human Flourishing Program at Harvard, and Byron R. Johnson, Director of the Institute for Studies of Religion at Baylor University. It measures global flourishing across six domains: happiness and life satisfaction, physical and mental health, meaning and purpose, character and virtue, close social relationships, and financial and material stability.

## Key Findings

### Global Differences in Flourishing

Many middle-income developing countries performed better in terms of meaning, purpose, and relationships than wealthier developed nations. Indonesia, Mexico, and the Philippines fared particularly well, while Japan, Turkey, and the United Kingdom did not score as favorably.

### Younger Generations Lagging

Younger people appear to be faring worse than older generations. Flourishing tends to increase with age in many countries, including Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Germany, Mexico, Spain, Sweden, the United Kingdom, and the United States. In many of these nations, the youngest age group (18–24 year olds) reported the lowest levels of flourishing.

### Spirituality is a Common Contributor

Across nearly all countries studied, religious service attendance appears to be an important element related to flourishing, with especially strong associations even in the most secular societies.

## Open Data and Longitudinal Design

The data is freely available to the public through the Center for Open Science. The principal investigators invite open dialogue and active contributions from the global research community and satellite locations. Beyond this First Wave, the GFS will continue tracking the same cohort over the course of five years.

## Study Scope and Methodology

The release of these findings represents more than four years of collaboration among over 40 leading experts who surveyed 200,000 research participants across more than 20 geographically and culturally diverse populations spanning all six populated continents. Countries included: Argentina, Australia, Brazil, China (Hong Kong), Egypt, Germany, India, Indonesia, Israel, Japan, Kenya, Mexico, Nigeria, the Philippines, Poland, Turkey, South Africa, Spain, Sweden, Tanzania, the United Kingdom, and the United States.

This $43.4 million initiative is supported by a consortium of funders including the John Templeton Foundation, Templeton Religion Trust, Templeton World Charity Foundation, Fetzer Institute, Paul Foster Family Foundation, Wellbeing for Planet Earth Foundation, Well Being Trust, and David & Carol Myers Foundation.
