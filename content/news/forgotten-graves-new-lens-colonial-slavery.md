*by Courtney Hayes*

Jason Ur, Stephen Phillips Professor of Archaeology and Ethnology and IQSS affiliate, is working to unearth histories long hidden in Cambridge. Known for pioneering archaeological work in the Middle East, Ur has turned his attention to Cambridge's Old Burying Ground, where questions about segregation and slavery have remained buried for centuries. Using drones, ground-penetrating radar, and a geospatial database, he is re-mapping the past and challenging how we understand it.

## From Iraq to Cambridge

Ur's early career focused on Iraq's ancient cities and empires, where traditional archaeological tools proved insufficient. "You can't dig an entire city, and you certainly can't dig an empire," he explained. To address this limitation, he turned to landscape archaeology, using geographic information systems (GIS) to tackle large-scale questions. A breakthrough came when the Clinton administration declassified Cold War satellite photos in the 1990s. "These images are like time machines," Ur says, describing how they reveal landscapes lost to urbanization and agriculture.

High-resolution Cold War imagery allowed Ur to trace ancient roadways, irrigation systems, and entire settlements, visualizing urban growth over millennia. "What's been really revolutionary," he noted, "is that this data is now in our pockets." By combining satellite photography with drone imagery, Ur and partners can visualize landscapes in ways unthinkable a decade ago. He notes that what once took weeks can now be accomplished by flying a drone over a site for ten minutes and processing the images through a smartphone app. These tools have deepened collaboration with local teams, democratizing archaeological research and strengthening international partnerships.

## A Pandemic Pivot

When the pandemic struck in 2020, Ur was unable to access field sites in Iraq. He began investigating Cambridge's Old Burying Ground, a cemetery dating to the 1630s that holds clues about New England's colonial past and its connections to slavery. Ur and students had visited this site for decades as part of an introductory Archaeology course. After the murder of George Floyd and a national reckoning on race, Ur began seeing the Old Burying Ground differently.

## Cicely and Jane

Ur began collaborating with Dr. Aja Lans, a postdoctoral fellow in the Inequality in American Initiative, and Andrew Bair, an advanced graduate student in the Anthropology Department's Archaeology program. They were drawn to two graves isolated on the site's remote northwest edge — headstones belonging to Cicely and Jane, enslaved Black women who worked in the homes of Harvard faculty in the early 1700s. Cicely was enslaved in the household of Reverend William Brattle, a Harvard tutor and minister, while Jane served in the household of Andrew Bordman, a Harvard bursar.

"As a landscape person, what struck me was their marginalization," said Ur. He noted that Jane and Cicely's headstones sit next to each other but far from the families that enslaved them, suggesting that even if segregation was not enforced during their lives, it was certainly imposed in death.

## Challenging Northern Myths

Slavery in the northern United States is often misunderstood, with many assuming it took a gentler form than southern plantation slavery. Ur's research reveals a system of segregation deeply entrenched in New England. He described the experience of being part of a community actively rethinking its own history, especially involvement with slavery, as "a really powerful moment."

Black residents of Colonial Cambridge are nearly invisible today, with only Cicely and Jane's headstones marking their graves. Records show that many more enslaved individuals lived and worked in Cambridge. Between the University's founding in 1636 and the abolition of slavery in the Commonwealth in 1783, Harvard faculty and staff enslaved more than 70 individuals. Ur suspected the Old Burying Ground might contain graves of many more enslaved persons who received no formal headstone.

## Spatial Methods and Discovery

To map the cemetery's complex layout, Ur deployed drones offering precise, high-resolution images. "It all starts with really good spatial control," he explained. "For me, that has to begin with remote sensing." Using photogrammetry software, he stitched thousands of aerial photographs into a detailed map, capturing each headstone's exact position, paired with dates, names, and iconographic details to build a geospatial database.

By filtering data to remove all graves postdating 1714 — the year Cicely died — Ur could visualize her isolated resting place at the cemetery's edge. He noted this isolation "would not have been apparent if I didn't have the ability to use those spatial attributes to clear off everything else."

Ur's team then used ground-penetrating radar to identify unmarked graves near Cicely and Jane's headstones. Based on their analysis, they believe there are almost 50 unmarked burials in that area, quite likely the burial place of many of Cambridge's enslaved dead.

## Scaling Across Greater Boston

The mapping of Cambridge's Old Burying Ground was only the beginning. Ur's work now examines burial patterns across Greater Boston. He observed that enslaved peoples' burials consistently follow a pattern of marginalization — downslope from ministers, in corners, surrounded by empty space, always set apart.

With over 20,000 monuments documented from 20 Boston-area cemeteries, Ur's dataset has outgrown his ability to manage it alone. IQSS helps him scale from field maps to an unprecedented geospatial dataset, making it possible to analyze spatial patterns and share work across disciplines. Ur remarked that archaeologists have historically been solo operators but "we really need the support that IQSS brings to handle monstrous data sets."

## Conclusion

Ur's journey from Iraq's landscapes to Greater Boston's burial grounds highlights archaeology's evolving nature. With IQSS's assistance, he can scale the conversation to encompass more voices and stories, offering a richer understanding of the past.
