*by Courtney Hayes*

On May 25, 2020, a Minneapolis police officer killed George Floyd by kneeling on his neck for almost 10 minutes. Floyd's death sparked the largest mass protest movement in U.S. history and a national reckoning on race and police reform.

In the fall of 2020, pressure to act was intensifying. Protestors called for radical reform while crime rates spiked. Between 2019 and 2020, the U.S. murder rate rose 30%—the largest single-year increase in more than a century. As election season approached, former President Donald Trump began blaming Democratic mayors.

The escalating calls for change and political finger pointing caught the attention of Justin de Benedictis-Kessner, a professor of public policy at the Harvard Kennedy School and a faculty affiliate at the Institute for Quantitative Social Science (IQSS). As he recalls, "a lot of the national political rhetoric around crime and policing was that Democrats wanted to defund the police" and that Democratic cities were becoming more dangerous. He and his colleagues wanted to determine whether this was actually attributable to Democratic leadership or merely an observational equivalence problem.

Previous research suggests mayors' ability to unilaterally effect change is often limited, and de Benedictis-Kessner questioned whether they could actually change anything.

In January 2022, de Benedictis-Kessner and co-authors Matthew Harvey, Daniel Jones, and Christopher Warshaw set out to examine these questions. Their study looks at whether a mayor's partisan affiliation might lead to differences in crime rates, arrest rates, and the racial composition of arrests.

It was an enormous undertaking. They collected three decades of election data and employed a regression discontinuity design to determine the causal effect of electing a Democratic rather than Republican mayor on policing and crime outcomes. As de Benedictis-Kessner noted, "We wanted to get around this in a causal way and say, what's the actual effect of electing different people?"

The research team faced many hurdles. While researchers have access to large data sets at the national level, data on elections, crime, and policing at the local level is more scarce. De Benedictis-Kessner explains that "it's really difficult to get accurate data on what's actually happening in terms of crime" and related local policing characteristics.

IQSS support proved invaluable here. De Benedictis-Kessner hired IQSS undergraduate research scholars who collected new data on local police department leaders.

Over a year, their research confirmed many initial expectations—mayoral partisanship does not affect overall crime rates, arrests, or police employment and expenditures.

However, surprises emerged. Partisanship can influence the racial distribution of arrests. De Benedictis-Kessner explains that "electing a Democrat mayor leads to proportionally fewer Black arrests for drug-related and 'other' uncategorized crimes." These differences are small—a two percentage point reduction in the Black share of drug arrests and a similar decline in the Black share of traffic stops and nuisance crimes where police have more discretion.

Mayors face many limiting constraints but do have power to appoint police chiefs who oversee staffing and local police culture. Data collected by de Benedictis-Kessner and his IQSS researchers suggests Democratic mayors may be more likely to hire nonwhite police chiefs. As he explains, background research in political science suggests benefits to descriptive representation—having police chiefs who look like the people they serve may help them better represent those communities' interests.

The impact of a diversified police force is hotly debated. The January 2023 killing of Tyre Nichols—brutalized by Black police officers in a city led by a Democratic mayor and an African American police chief—illustrates a vexing reality. Police reform is slow and the role of politics is often limited.

But de Benedictis-Kessner insists who we elect matters. Even a small change in the share of Black arrests is significant because "every single additional person who's arrested by the police adds the potential for use of force" and tragic incidents like Nichols' death.

In fall 2022, de Benedictis-Kessner and co-authors began presenting findings at universities across the country. Feedback from fellow scholars was clear—they needed to know who police chiefs are, what they look like, and where they come from.

These questions go to the heart of what motivates de Benedictis-Kessner. He works on local government because "we should know these answers about this level of government, and we just don't know who's in these offices."

The challenge of collecting those data initially seemed insurmountable, but the IQSS research assistants "made quick work of actually getting this police chiefs data." He noted he couldn't have collected 10,000 rows of data in two months without the IQSS undergrads.

Collecting such detailed information on American police chiefs has never been done before. De Benedictis-Kessner notes the significance: previously, researchers couldn't answer basic questions about whether police chiefs look like the people they serve—information hugely important for assessing representational quality.

The study also highlights the impact of distorted rhetoric and disinformation on journalists, voters, and policy makers. De Benedictis-Kessner explains that "the rhetoric, especially in the media, but also coming directly from politicians, is really detached from the reality on crime."

He credits IQSS with playing a vital role in "translating social science evidence and complicated statistics into something that the average reporter and voter can understand" in ways that inform policy debates.

For de Benedictis-Kessner and his co-authors, IQSS has expanded their idea of what's possible in urban policy research. He describes it as "a fantastic group of people that centralizes a lot of the quantitative social science work that's happening at Harvard," noting how helpful it is to have people supporting the research mission while creating community.
