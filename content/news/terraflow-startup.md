Dan Freeman, a Harvard PhD student in Biology and Biomedical Sciences, had prior experience with single-cell technology and pharma trials. Convinced there was a better approach, he began experimenting with artificial intelligence and arrived at his breakthrough concept. His vision involved using AI to accelerate drug discovery, and he had developed an early prototype. What he needed was guidance on commercialization—identifying customers, refining his pitch, and building a business plan. The IQSS Startup Foundry provided that support.

terraFlow emerged from a straightforward but bold premise: merging pharmaceutical companies' internal data with the enormous body of scientific literature to identify biologically meaningful biomarkers. In drug development, these signals can determine whether a trial fails or produces a life-saving therapy. Freeman's technology aimed to accelerate that process by analyzing millions of research papers and connecting them to patient outcomes.

## Learning to Speak the Language of Customers

Through the Startup Foundry, Dan worked with industry, technology, and business experts. He learned to pivot from discussing algorithmic sophistication to articulating the problems his tool solved for pharma scientists and executives. Structured customer discovery exercises helped him book more conversations, improve his questions, and reshape his messaging. As he recalls: "The question isn't just what the AI can do, but how it helps someone hit their clinical milestones faster."

## From Pitch to Pre-Seed Success

The narrative refinement paid dividends. With a sharpened story and coaching from Foundry mentors, Freeman pitched investors and closed a $750,000 pre-seed round, with $600,000 coming from Boston-based Underscore VC. The capital provided 18 months of runway tied to three milestones: launching terraFlow 2.0, closing enterprise deals, and reaching positive cash flow.

The company debuted at the international CYTO conference in Edinburgh, where Dan and his team unveiled terraFlow 2.0 to an audience of scientists and industry leaders. Shortly after, the startup began piloting its AI with AstraZeneca, Vertex, Regeneron, Boehringer Ingelheim, and Eli Lilly. By the following quarter, terraFlow had doubled its active demos, deployed its platform inside AstraZeneca's clinical trial datasets, and built a sales pipeline exceeding $600,000.

## Turning Science into Impact

terraFlow also reinforced its competitive moat by mining 100,000 scientific papers (targeting one million), filing two U.S. patents, and establishing itself as an industry player. Alongside customer calls and enterprise pilots, Dan pitched on larger stages—most notably at Harvard Business School's Shark Tank, where he faced off against Kevin O'Leary, "Mr. Wonderful."

The path from idea to venture-backed company is seldom linear. For Dan, the Foundry's mentors and experts served as a compass through early uncertainty: helping him listen to customers, craft a compelling story, and build investor confidence. Today, with institutional backing, industry pilots, and growing ARR, terraFlow demonstrates that combining science, storytelling, and support can transform a bold idea into a company capable of changing how drugs are developed.

Dan explains: "The Startup Foundry helped me connect the dots. I had the science, but they showed me how to shape it into something customers wanted."
