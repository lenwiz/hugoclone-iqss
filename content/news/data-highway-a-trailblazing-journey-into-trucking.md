*by Courtney Hayes*

As highways become a new digital landscape where every truck serves as a node and every delivery a connection, a Harvard PhD-turned-entrepreneur is helping build tomorrow's transportation infrastructure. IQSS affiliate Jinyan Zang, founder and COO of Axle, has created a data platform that seeks to transform trucking logistics.

The trucking industry today is massive. With almost four million tractor-trailers on US highways, trucking accounts for over 70% of the nation's freight. The internet now connects every truck in the United States with the potential to track fuel levels, speed, location, and more. But the industry is highly fragmented, with thousands of brokers and dispatchers working around the clock to manage siloed data streams. As a result, up to 30% of all vehicles are driving empty. Zang notes: "That's burning a ton of diesel without actually doing any work. That's money out of the driver's and shipper's pocket."

Zang and his team at Axle are working to make trucking faster, cheaper, and greener by wrangling hundreds of data sources into a neutral platform. "All these different systems that track the vehicles and drivers have to be able to talk to one another," says Zang. Rather than pushing a single software solution, his team is creating a standardized platform on which tech companies can build new ways to optimize deliveries and reduce fuel costs. "We are the glue that holds everything together. And I think that's what makes Axle really unique," Zang explains.

## The Path from Harvard to Trucking

When Zang arrived on campus as an undergrad in 2009, Harvard Square was transformed into a movie set for *The Social Network*. As Zang recalls, "Everyone wanted to be the next Mark Zuckerberg." The prevailing campus sentiment was that technology could do no wrong.

In his senior year, Zang took a computer science class that challenged those assumptions and changed the course of his life. The class was taught by Latanya Sweeney, founding director of the IQSS Data Privacy Lab and a professor of government and technology at Harvard. Sweeney is a pioneer in data privacy, and her class examined technology's clash with society.

The IQSS Data Privacy Lab began in 2011 as concerns about unauthorized online data collection were gaining momentum. After graduating from Harvard in 2013, Zang joined Sweeney at IQSS, where they conducted groundbreaking research on the privacy of genetic data. Sweeney's work is now cited in two US regulations, including HIPAA.

In 2014, Sweeney took a leave from Harvard to serve as the US Federal Trade Commission's Chief Technology Officer and hired Zang as one of the first technology fellows. At the FTC, Zang worked on data privacy policies and consumer protection issues.

When Zang returned to Harvard to pursue his PhD in 2015, he continued his work with Sweeney as a fellow at IQSS. His research documented the potential for civil rights violations by Facebook. His findings were part of a Department of Justice investigation that helped change policies around Facebook's discriminatory ad targeting.

## From COVID Contact Tracing to Axle

In 2020, when COVID-19 shut down campuses, Zang and Sweeney built Harvard's contact tracing platform. "We were really diving deep in terms of building technology that had to be scalable," Zang explains, noting it's exactly what they're doing at Axle but in a different sector.

At that time, Zang reconnected with old classmate and mobility expert Dhruv Gupta, and their vision for a logistics startup began to percolate. They spent the winter of 2021 tinkering with Zang's Honda CRV, exploring what telematics data they could pull from vehicles. "We quickly saw a huge opportunity in trucking logistics. And that was the light bulb moment for us."

## Privacy at the Core

In two short years, Axle has grown to seven employees with thousands of vehicles using their platform. One of their biggest challenges relates to data privacy—securely collecting sensitive data while protecting users' privacy rights. "Not only are there machines involved, but drivers too. So, it's an opt-in system," Zang explains.

Now in the private sector, Zang finds himself on the other side of the conversation he helped shape at IQSS. "We're a startup building a giant data platform. So now I get to draw on the experience I gained at IQSS." Obtaining informed consent, respecting users' rights, and anonymizing data where possible are among the best practices that startups like Axle embrace.

Despite numerous proposals, there has yet to be a comprehensive federal law governing data privacy in the US. Private sector leaders are beginning to recognize the importance of rebuilding trust. Zang observes: "there's so much you can do when you have trust, when your people are willing to share data about themselves."

Drawing insights from past mistakes in tech and helping shape balanced solutions is where IQSS plays a critical role. "We're building that future trying to address privacy and fixing what hasn't worked in the past," says Zang. For him and his team at Axle, the road ahead offers boundless potential.
