A survey led by faculty affiliate Stephen Ansolabehere, the Elting R. Morison Professor of Political Science and Professor of Government at Harvard, explores what Americans prioritize regarding large data centers opening in their communities. The research found that local community concerns matter more to residents than electricity pricing when it comes to data center siting decisions, challenging conventional assumptions about public priorities in infrastructure development.

As demand for data centers surges driven by cloud computing, artificial intelligence, and digital services, companies and policymakers have often focused on electricity costs and power grid capacity as primary factors in location decisions. However, Ansolabehere's research reveals that community members place greater emphasis on factors such as environmental impacts, effects on local character and quality of life, job creation, property values, and how well the facility integrates with existing community fabric. These "local vibes"—the intangible qualities that define a community's identity and lived experience—emerge as more salient to residents than the economic considerations that dominate industry planning.

The findings have important implications for data center developers, local governments, and policymakers navigating the complex politics of infrastructure siting. Understanding that communities care deeply about preserving local character and managing impacts beyond economics suggests that successful data center projects will need to engage more meaningfully with community concerns and design facilities that are sensitive to local contexts. The research contributes to broader conversations about infrastructure development, community engagement, and the social dimensions of technological change.
