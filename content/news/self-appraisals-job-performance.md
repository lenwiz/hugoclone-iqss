Faculty affiliate Iris Bohnet, Albert Pratt Professor of Business and Government at Harvard Kennedy School and director of the Women and Public Policy Program, has published new research challenging the widespread practice of using self-appraisals in workplace performance evaluations. Her recent paper examines how worker performance ratings are affected by various biases and structural factors that can undermine the accuracy and fairness of self-assessment processes.

Bohnet's research reveals that self-appraisals, while seemingly empowering to employees, often introduce systematic distortions into performance evaluation systems. The study found that self-ratings are influenced by factors including individual confidence levels, cultural backgrounds, and gender, with some groups systematically underrating their performance while others tend toward inflation. These patterns can inadvertently perpetuate workplace inequalities, as more modest self-assessors may be disadvantaged in compensation and promotion decisions even when their actual performance is strong.

The findings have important implications for human resources practices and organizational design. Bohnet's work suggests that companies relying heavily on self-appraisals may need to reconsider their performance management systems and explore alternative approaches that reduce bias while still giving employees voice in the evaluation process. The research contributes to ongoing efforts to make workplace assessments more objective and equitable, particularly for women and underrepresented groups who may be disadvantaged by current self-appraisal practices.
