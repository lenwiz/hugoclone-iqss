IQSS affiliate Christina Cross, Associate Professor of Sociology at Harvard University, has published a book that critically examines one of the most persistent claims in debates about racial inequality: that promoting two-parent nuclear families is a simple solution to closing racial gaps in economic and social outcomes. Through rigorous analysis of demographic and economic data, Cross challenges this conventional wisdom and reveals the limitations of family structure as an explanation for racial disparities.

Cross's research demonstrates that even when controlling for family structure, substantial racial inequalities persist across multiple domains including income, wealth, educational outcomes, and social mobility. Black children in two-parent households face different opportunity structures and social contexts than their white counterparts in similar family configurations, reflecting the ongoing impacts of structural racism, residential segregation, labor market discrimination, and differential access to high-quality schools and other resources. The research shows that simplistic prescriptions about family structure ignore these deeper structural factors that drive inequality.

The book makes an important intervention in both academic and policy discussions about racial inequality. By demonstrating that family structure changes alone cannot eliminate racial gaps, Cross redirects attention toward the structural and institutional factors that create and maintain inequality. Her work has significant implications for policy debates, suggesting that efforts to promote family formation, while potentially valuable for other reasons, should not be mistaken for comprehensive solutions to racial inequality. Instead, addressing persistent racial disparities requires confronting discrimination, segregation, and unequal access to opportunities across multiple domains of social and economic life.
