*by Jonathan Colburn*

**Changing Seasons: Kosuke Imai Returns to Harvard to Expand the Reach of Quantitative Social Science at Harvard and Abroad**

When he last occupied office space at Harvard, Kosuke Imai, IQSS's newest resident faculty affiliate, had a decidedly different experience than he does in his current location at CGIS Knafel overlooking Cambridge Street.

"IQSS didn't exist yet, but its predecessor was housed in a little yellow house where the Ukrainian Research Institute is," Imai recalls. His graduate student office was upstairs in the attic, and demolition of the old building nearby meant that construction equipment would shake his entire workspace.

His working conditions are just the beginning of changes since returning to Harvard after completing a Ph.D. in Political Science in 2003 and an M.A. in Statistics in 2002. After spending 15 years as a professor at Princeton, Imai rejoined Harvard as a professor appointed jointly in the Departments of Government and of Statistics, and as an affiliate of IQSS. His return has opened up collaborative opportunities across Harvard and the myriad research institutions in the greater Boston area.

"My work has always been in developing statistical methods, but the methods themselves are applicable across different problems and social science fields," Imai explains. He was seeking more interaction beyond political science, including with professional schools like HSPH and the Law School.

This search led to a collaboration with Jim Greiner, a professor at Harvard Law School and Faculty Director of the Access to Justice Lab. Greiner had an interest in evaluating risk assessment scores used by criminal courts. These algorithms serve various purposes within the criminal justice system, such as helping determine whether individuals charged with a crime should be released until trial or held due to high risk of committing further crimes or failing to attend hearings. Such algorithms are pervasive in modern life, affecting hiring decisions and university admissions. Questions about whether these algorithms can be biased against individuals based on ethnicity, gender, income status, or other factors have spawned entire fields of research on algorithmic fairness. But beyond score fairness lies a further question of how people choose to use them in decision-making.

"What interests me is that there are human judges making these decisions, not just following the numbers," Imai says. He draws a parallel to doctors receiving algorithmic information—both judges and doctors ultimately make their own decisions. The scores may be biased, and humans have their own biases, and these may interact in ways not yet understood.

To help provide answers, Greiner convinced some states to participate in a study where some judges have access to risk scores while making bail decisions and some do not. Imai and collaborators developed new statistical methods for assessing the impacts of machine recommendations on human decision making, and they have just started analyzing data. The road to conclusive results requires assumptions—it is impossible to know what choices individuals denied bail would make if released—but the research may lead to more equitable policy outcomes. "There is a lot of research on determining the most optimal, or fair, score, but the most fair score may not lead to the most fair decision by a judge," Imai notes.

The project is emblematic of a whole world of possible collaborations for Imai, whose research has also covered evaluation of congressional redistricting, software development for municipalities merging voter records, and analyzing election impacts in states where inactive voters are purged from rolls. His return to New England has brought more than expanded research opportunities.

"I've been biking to my office from Newton most days," Imai shares. He started when the weather was nice and wasn't sure he could make it through winter, but succeeded. He bikes along the Charles River and finds it beautiful in all four seasons. Originally from Japan, he appreciates the changing seasons.

His two teenage children have adjusted to their new community, helped by the fact that annual family ski trips to Vermont are now a few hours shorter.

He has also pursued a multi-year project in partnership with the University of Tokyo, where he teaches for a month every year to train young researchers and help build a quantitative scholarly community.

"Quantitative social science is a very active area of research in the United States, but in Asia the scientific study of society is still not a major area of focus," Imai explains. Research there tends to be more qualitative or historical rather than using data to scientifically evaluate hypotheses, though interest exists—everyone is excited about data.

In addition to his course, he helps lead an annual Asian Political Methodology Meeting. He admits it has taken time getting used to sitting in department meetings with faculty peers who once served as his instructors. But having seen results of his teaching firsthand, Imai hopes some of his own students may join him in turn.

He has students from Japan, Korea, and elsewhere who took his class and then came to Harvard as graduate students. He acknowledges his impact may feel small-scale in some ways, but believes that training one person who then trains others allows him to see the impact grow over time.
