The Human Flourishing Program at Harvard University has made a landmark contribution to social science research with the public release of both Wave 1 and Wave 2 data from the Global Flourishing Study. This massive dataset includes responses from roughly 200,000 participants across 22 countries, making it one of the most comprehensive longitudinal studies of human well-being ever conducted. The data covers six fundamental domains of flourishing: happiness and life satisfaction, physical and mental health, meaning and purpose, character and virtue, close social relationships, and financial and material stability.

The availability of two waves of longitudinal data opens unprecedented opportunities for researchers to examine how flourishing changes over time, both at individual and population levels. Scholars can now track trajectories of well-being, identify factors that predict increases or decreases in flourishing, and explore how different life events and circumstances affect various dimensions of well-being across diverse cultural contexts. The study's geographic and cultural breadth—spanning countries across all six populated continents—enables meaningful cross-cultural comparisons and insights into universal versus culture-specific aspects of human flourishing.

The Global Flourishing Study represents a collaborative effort among Harvard's Human Flourishing Program, Baylor's Institute for Studies of Religion, Gallup, and the Center for Open Science, supported by more than $43 million in funding from multiple foundations. All data is freely available through the Center for Open Science, reflecting the project's commitment to open science principles. As the study continues to track participants over a five-year period, it will become an even more valuable resource for understanding the dynamics of human well-being and informing policies and interventions aimed at promoting flourishing worldwide.
