Research from IQSS scholars examines a provocative question in debates about inequality and family structure: does marriage and the two-parent family serve as a great equalizer, helping to reduce economic and social disparities? This question sits at the intersection of research on family structure, economic mobility, and social stratification, with important implications for how we understand pathways out of poverty and the role of family policy in addressing inequality.

The analysis explores whether children from disadvantaged backgrounds experience better outcomes when raised in two-parent households, potentially narrowing gaps with more advantaged peers. The research employs rigorous quantitative methods to disentangle correlation from causation, addressing the challenge that families with two parents may differ in many ways from single-parent families beyond just family structure. By carefully examining patterns across different measures of child well-being and adult outcomes, the work contributes evidence to ongoing policy debates about marriage promotion, family support programs, and anti-poverty strategies.

The findings have important implications for both scholarship and policy. Understanding the relationship between family structure and inequality requires nuanced analysis that avoids both romanticizing traditional family forms and dismissing the real challenges that single parents face in providing for their children. The research contributes to more sophisticated understanding of how family contexts shape children's life chances and what factors might genuinely help families across all structures support their children's flourishing. This work exemplifies IQSS's commitment to bringing rigorous quantitative analysis to questions with profound social and policy significance.
